TITLE: Flexible, dual-form nicotine replacement therapy or varenicline in comparison with nicotine patch for smoking cessation: a randomized controlled trial
AUTHORS AND AFFILIATIONS:
- Heather E. Tulloch - Division of Prevention and Rehabilitation, University of Ottawa Heart Institute; Faculty of Medicine, University of Ottawa; School of Psychology, University of Ottawa
- Andrew L. Pipe - Division of Prevention and Rehabilitation, University of Ottawa Heart Institute; Faculty of Medicine, University of Ottawa
- Charl Els - Department of Psychiatry, University of Alberta
- Matthew J. Clyde - Division of Prevention and Rehabilitation, University of Ottawa Heart Institute; School of Psychology, University of Ottawa
- Robert D. Reid - Division of Prevention and Rehabilitation, University of Ottawa Heart Institute; Faculty of Medicine, University of Ottawa
TRIAL REGISTRATION: NCT01623505 (ClinicalTrials.gov; retrospectively registered July 13, 2011)
FUNDING: Heart and Stroke Foundation of Canada (Grant #6614)

METHODS:
A parallel three-group randomized controlled trial conducted at the University of Ottawa Heart Institute (June 2010-July 2014). Study enrolled 737 smokers (>=10 cigarettes/day, willing to quit within 2-4 weeks) with medical and psychiatric comorbidities. Participants randomized to: NRT (10-week nicotine patch, max 21 mg/day); NRT+ (flexible combination patches up to 35 mg/day plus gum/inhalers, up to 22 weeks); or varenicline (1 mg twice-daily, up to 24 weeks post-target quit date). All groups received six standardized 15-minute nurse-led counseling sessions. Primary outcome: carbon monoxide-confirmed continuous abstinence (weeks 5-52). Secondary outcomes: abstinence at weeks 5-10, 5-22; 7-day point prevalence at weeks 10, 22, 52. Intention-to-treat analysis using logistic regression; adjusted analyses controlled for baseline predictors.

RESULTS:
Continuous abstinence rates (weeks 5-52): NRT 10.0%, NRT+ 12.4%, varenicline 15.3% (no significant differences). Using 7-day point prevalence at week 52, varenicline was superior to NRT (adjusted OR 1.84; 97.5% CI 1.04-3.26). Varenicline showed higher abstinence at weeks 5-22 versus NRT (OR 2.01; CI 1.20-3.36). Both NRT+ and varenicline increased early success (weeks 5-10) compared to NRT. Extended treatment (beyond 10 weeks) showed benefit in medium-term outcomes. Varenicline users reported more fatigue, digestive symptoms, and sleep disturbances; fewer dermatologic symptoms. Serious adverse events did not differ between groups.

EDIA/EQUITY REPORTING:
