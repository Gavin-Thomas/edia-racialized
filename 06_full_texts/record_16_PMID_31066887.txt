TITLE: Efficacy of Adjunctive Infliximab vs Placebo in the Treatment of Adults With Bipolar I/II Depression: A Randomized Clinical Trial

METHODS:
Study Design: 12-week, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, parallel-group, fixed-dose clinical trial evaluating efficacy, safety, and tolerability of adjunctive infliximab for treatment of individuals with DSM-5-defined bipolar disorder I/II depression who met inflammatory criteria. Conducted at Mood Disorders Psychopharmacology Unit, University Health Network, Toronto, Ontario, Canada, and Department of Psychiatry and Behavioral Sciences, Stanford University, Palo Alto, California. Approved by Institutional Ethics Board at University Health Network and Stanford University.

Participants: Outpatients aged 18-65 years with bipolar disorder meeting DSM-5 criteria for a current major depressive episode. Required MADRS total scores >=22 and YMRS <12. Receipt of at least 2 treatments for bipolar disorder for minimum 4 weeks before randomization. Required to meet inflammatory criteria at baseline: CRP >=5 mg/L; or obesity with metabolic abnormalities; type 1 or 2 diabetes; inflammatory bowel disorder; rheumatologic disorder; daily cigarette smoking; or migraine headaches. Diagnoses confirmed by MINI 5.0.0 and SCID for DSM-5.

Intervention: Participants randomized to adjunctive IV infliximab (5 mg/kg) or placebo. Infusions administered for 120 minutes at weeks 0, 2, and 6 by registered nurse in rheumatology. Infliximab and placebo matched in color and consistency, prepared by hospital pharmacists with no contact with participants. Block randomization in blocks of 6.

Outcomes: Primary outcome was baseline-to-endpoint change in MADRS total score. Exploratory analyses evaluated moderating effects of baseline CRP, illness severity, and childhood maltreatment (Childhood Trauma Questionnaire). Response operationalized as >=50% decrease in MADRS; remission as MADRS <=12.

Statistical Analysis: Modified intent-to-treat analysis including all who received >=1 infusion and completed >=1 post-baseline assessment. GEE with negative binomial models. Age and sex as covariates. Moderators analyzed in separate models with Bonferroni adjustment. No interim analysis conducted.

RESULTS:
Demographics: 60 participants (55 from MDPU Toronto, 5 from Stanford) randomized to infliximab (n = 29; mean age 45.0 [11.7]; 71% female) or placebo (n = 31; mean age 46.8 [10.2]; 87% female). 47 participants (78%) completed all 12 weeks. No statistically significant sociodemographic or clinical differences between groups.

Safety: Infliximab generally well tolerated. Three moderate allergic reactions. One participant with liver function abnormalities dismissed. One participant developed psychosis on placebo and was hospitalized. One death (anoxic brain injury approximately 4 weeks after third infliximab infusion at Stanford site) deemed not study related.

Antidepressant Efficacy: Significant time effects and treatment x time interactions but no group effects. Treatment x time interaction significant only at week 2 (RR 0.86; P = .024); infliximab lower depressive severity at week 2 but not at weeks 6, 8, or 12. No significant time x treatment x CRP or clinical severity interactions. Significant time x treatment x CTQ total score effect (P = .02), strongly associated with physical abuse subdomain (P < .001). Higher physical abuse associated with higher response to infliximab at weeks 8 and 12. No between-group differences in response or remission at week 12.
