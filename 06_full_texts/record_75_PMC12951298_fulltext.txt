Abstract

Citalopram is effective in treating agitation in Alzheimer’s dementia (AD), but it is associated with cognitive and cardiac risks, likely due to its R -enantiomer. Escitalopram, the S -enantiomer, may be an alternative. In this double-masked randomized (1:1) placebo-controlled trial, we assessed the efficacy and safety of escitalopram in treating agitation in AD after failure of a psychosocial intervention (PSI). Assessments occurred at enrollment, post-PSI (baseline) and at 3, 6, 9 and 12 weeks post-baseline. Settings were 27 community-based centers. The target randomization sample was 392 participants. Participants were adults with AD, a Mini-Mental State Examination Telephone score of 3–20 and significant agitation. PSI non-responders received escitalopram (up to 15 mg per day) or placebo for 12 weeks while continuing PSI. The outcome was the proportion of participants with clinically significant improvement in agitation from baseline at 12 weeks. In total, 173 participants were randomized (84 escitalopram versus 89 placebo; mean ± s.d. age = 78.4 ± 8.7 years; 90 men (52.0%); 127 White (73.4%)). The unadjusted risk difference at 12 weeks was 0.08 (95% confidence interval: −0.21, 0.06). Drug-related QT interval prolongation was observed. Although the randomized sample was smaller than planned, escitalopram was not effective in treating agitation in AD and was associated with cardiac conduction delays. Clinicians need to be cautious in recommending escitalopram as an alternative to citalopram for this condition. ClincialTrials.gov identifier: NCT03108846 .

Agitation in Alzheimer’s dementia (AD) is a common burden for patients, caregivers and the public at large 1 – 3 . It affects up to 60% of patients with AD, particularly in middle and later stages 4 – 9 . Current evidence-based treatments for agitation in AD demonstrate limited efficacy. Non-pharmacological treatments are recommended as first-line interventions 10 – 12 . Pharmacological treatments are indicated when agitation is not responsive to non-pharmacological interventions alone or to optimize their efficacy 10 – 13 .

Antipsychotic medications are widely used, especially in severe agitation 10 . They include risperidone, quetiapine and brexpiprazole, which is the only medication with US Food & Drug Administration (FDA) marketing approval for agitation in AD. However, antipsychotics have modest efficacy and are associated with sedation, falls, extrapyramidal symptoms, cerebrovascular accidents and death 14 , 15 . Notwithstanding concerns regarding their safety or efficacy, other medications used in the management of agitation include anticonvulsants, antidepressants, benzodiazepines, dextromethorphan (in a proprietary combination with a metabolism inhibitor) and cognitive enhancers 16 .

Citalopram, a selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor (SSRI), at a dose of 30 mg per day, was efficacious and safe in treating agitation in AD 17 – 19 in a multicenter randomized controlled trial (RCT) conducted by our group (Citalopram for Agitation in Alzheimer’s Disease Study (CitAD)), with 40% of the 94 participants randomized to citalopram experiencing clinically meaningful improvement compared to 26% of the 92 placebo-treated participants, with clinical meaningfulness defined as marked or moderate improvement on the modified Alzheimer Disease Cooperative Study-Clinical Global Impression of Change (mADCS-CGIC) 20 . This dose is higher than the upper limit of 20 mg, as recommended by the FDA in older adults 21 . CitAD also showed that citalopram efficacy was higher among community-dwelling patients than those living in care homes 22 ; that risks for QTc prolongation and cognitive decline were associated with the R -enantiomer of citalopram 23 ; and that efficacy was associated with the S -enantiomer 23 . These findings should limit the widespread use of citalopram as a treatment for agitation in AD and show promise for examining the efficacy of escitalopram, an FDA-approved generic SSRI containing only the S -enantiomer of citalopram. CitAD also demonstrated that 26% (ref. 24 ) of participants randomized to placebo combined with a brief, standardized and scalable psychosocial intervention (PSI) experienced clinically meaningful improvement in agitation, with most of the improvement being observed by 3 weeks of treatment 25 .

With the above background, we designed 26 and completed a double-masked, multicenter RCT to test the efficacy and safety of a 12-week course of escitalopram versus placebo—both combined with PSI—for agitation in community-dwelling adults with AD after a 3-week course of PSI alone ( Fig. 1 ). The primary hypothesis was that the proportion of participants in the escitalopram + PSI group demonstrating clinically significant improvement of agitation would be larger than the corresponding proportion in the placebo + PSI group 26 .

Results

Patient disposition

Of 243 participants consented and assessed for eligibility between 14 March 2018 and 30 November 2023, 205 were eligible and enrolled in the study. This included 32 participants who exited the study before completing the PSI-alone run-in phase, who either responded to standardized PSI alone or were ineligible to be randomized ( Fig. 2 ; CONSORT diagram). Thus, 173 participants were randomized between 10 April 2018 and 31 December 2023 to escitalopram for 12 weeks + standardized PSI ( n = 84) or placebo for 12 weeks + standardized PSI ( n = 89), with a mean ± s.d. age of 78.4 ± 8.7 years, 90 men (52.0%) and 127 White (73.4%) ( Extended Data Tables 1 and 2 ). The total randomized sample was lower than our target sample of 392 participants.

Sensitivity analyses

The per-protocol analysis and all sensitivity analyses did not yield any significant RDs between the two treatment groups ( Extended Data Tables 4 and 5 and Extended Data Fig. 1 ).

Post hoc analyses

Given the smaller-than-planned sample size and to lend further support to our findings, we conducted analyses to assess the results had the full planned sample size been achieved. At a sample size of n = 390 (rounded down from n = 392 to account for the fact that exact calculations require whole numbers of participants), the CI for our observed RD would have been (−0.1670, 0.0175) with a non-significant P value. The estimated power at this sample size is 0.36. The sample size needed to have 80% power for the Escitalopram for Agitation in Alzheimer’s Disease (S-CitAD) observed response of 8% RD is n = 1,048.

Discussion

The primary results of this phase 3, double-masked, multicenter RCT do not support the efficacy of escitalopram + PSI in treating agitation in AD after a 3-week course of PSI alone. No difference was observed between the two treatment arms in the proportion of participants who experienced marked or moderate improvement in agitation at 12 weeks compared to BL. The lack of evidence for a treatment effect persisted after adjusting for clinical site, in the per-protocol analysis including only participants who completed all 12 weeks of follow-up and in sensitivity analyses including one adjusting for enrollment before versus after coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19).

The percentage of participants who were randomized to placebo and experienced marked or moderate improvement in agitation at 12 weeks was 27%. This is congruent with what we predicted in our power analysis and consistent with the results of CitAD, in which 26% improved in this arm 24 . In contrast, the percentage of participants who were randomized to escitalopram and experienced marked or moderate improvement in agitation at 12 weeks was 34.5%, which is lower than the 40% observed in the citalopram + PSI group in CitAD 19 .

One key difference in study design between CitAD and S-CitAD is the 3-week PSI run-in in advance of administering the study drug. We consider it unlikely that the introduction of this design feature played a role in the unexpectedly low efficacy on escitalopram, and, in fact, by eliminating PSI-only responders, this should serve to diminish placebo response. Rather, this might suggest that the efficacy of escitalopram is lower than that of citalopram at similar doses. However, the studies occurred in very different time periods, with widespread use of citalopram providing challenges to recruitment into S-CitAD. We speculate that a subset of responders to serotonergic medications may have been systematically excluded from the S-CitAD recruitment pool due to treatment with citalopram. Hence, although this is a speculation, this study likely enrolled a different population than CitAD, possibly with more individuals whom their clinicians did not think that citalopram or escitalopram was the right choice for them, otherwise they would have tried them. We also note that, due to serious challenges with recruitment, this study was underpowered to detect the effect size seen in CitAD and even more so to detect a 34% to 27% difference in responder rates. Smaller than expected cell sizes likely resulted in lower estimate precision, wider CIs and greater difficulty discriminating between drug and placebo effects. For more comparisons between CitAD and S-CitAD, see Extended Data Table 9 .

Our finding of a slightly higher proportion of participants with marked or moderate response to escitalopram over placebo at 3 weeks after randomization (that is, after failing to respond to PSI alone) is consistent with an augmenting effect of escitalopram when PSI is not sufficient. After the 3-week post-randomization, response rates in the two treatment groups converged, suggesting that the placebo + PSI group ‘catches up’ 3 weeks later. The pattern of change in agitation + aggression scores on NPI is also consistent with this accelerated response in the escitalopram + PSI group versus the placebo + PSI group. This pattern of change contrasts with CitAD where the separation between drug and placebo increased over time, in particular late in the study.

The difference in efficacy between CitAD and S-CitAD could also be due to the possible benefit from racemic citalopram for the treatment of agitation in AD. The R -enantiomer is 30-fold less potent than the S -enantiomer at inhibiting serotonin reuptake 30 . Furthermore, evidence suggests that the R -enantiomer inhibits 31 , 32 the response to the S -enantiomer, resulting in escitalopram having better antidepressant response and fewer side effects versus citalopram 33 . However, in treating agitation in AD, this suppressive effect of the R -enantiomer may be beneficial in treating agitation in AD, as the S -enantiomer on its own may cause more activation than the racemic mixture in patients with AD.

The treatment effects on MMSET are somewhat different than those of CitAD. Here, we did not observe a significant negative treatment effect at 12 weeks on MMSET: MMSET scores were relatively stable on escitalopram in this study, in contrast to a decline in MMSE scores on citalopram in CitAD. In both studies, the placebo groups had improved cognitive scores, possibly due to practice effects. Thus, although escitalopram may have fewer adverse cognitive effects than citalopram, it could still be suppressing practice effects. More detailed analyses using blood levels and detailed cognitive assessments will follow in future publications.

A key limitation of this study is the fact that only 44.1% of the target sample was recruited. However, post hoc analyses showed that the required sample to detect our observed effect would have needed to be 2–3 times larger than our target sample, raising considerable doubt about any clinical meaningfulness of such an effect. This is in contrast to the statistically and clinically significant effect observed in the citalopram study. Another limitation is the use of Bazett’s formula to correct QTc given that it is not accurate in patients with tachycardia or bradycardia. However, its use in our study was not problematic because, at enrollment, all participants had normal sinus rhythm and a heart rate within the normal range, and the vast majority of them continued to be so at follow-ups ( Extended Data Table 6 ). A third limitation is that we did not use biomarker-based diagnosis because we aimed to mirror, as much as possible, routine clinical practice given the potential immediate clinical implications of our results. Also, biomarker testing in this population as a whole would have been challenging or even impossible, but this should not discourage clinicians from using such testing when possible in individual patients in their practice. A strength of this study is the thorough and systematic assessment of behavioral outcomes and very good retention ( Fig. 2 ).

In conclusion, our results provide no reason to think that escitalopram is superior to citalopram, and, in fact, it might be inferior. Taken together with the increased rate of falls, the prolongation of QTcB and the increased incidence of diarrhea, although escitalopram might have less of a negative impact on cognition, our results suggest that citalopram should be the preferred choice over escitalopram for the treatment of agitation in AD when an SSRI is indicated.

Methods

Overall design

 Figure 1 shows the overall study design. In brief, after eligibility and enrollment (EN) assessments, participants received 3 weeks of PSI alone. At the end of the 3 weeks, agitation was reassessed (BL visit), and only those participants who did not experience response relative to EN, and who remained eligible for drug treatment, were randomized (1:1) to receive escitalopram or placebo for 12 weeks concomitant with PSI. Participants who responded to PSI alone, or who were not suitable for escitalopram treatment (primarily due to cardiac concerns), continued to be monitored on usual care for up to 12 weeks while receiving PSI. In addition to EN and BL assessments, all participants were assessed at weeks 3, 6, 9 and 12.

Participants

Participants were men and women aged 18 years and older who met the following key inclusion criteria (for more details, see our methods publication 26 ): (1) AD diagnosed according to the National Institute on Aging (NIA) and the Alzheimer’s Association (AA) (2011 NIA/AA criteria) 34 —thus, we assumed that the participants had probable AD without evidence of AD pathology; (2) MMSET score of 3–20 inclusive 35 , corresponding to a score of 5–28 inclusive on the Mini Mental State Examination (MMSE) 36 . We chose the MMSE to be consistent with the CitAD study, which was the basis for this trial. We then decided to switch to MMSET due to COVID-19. Moreover, at the dementia severity range that our participants were enrolled at, the MMSE is more appropriate than the Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA), which is difficult for anyone with moderate dementia to complete, let alone a population of agitated patients; (3) meeting International Psychogeriatric Association (IPA) provisional criteria for agitation in cognitive disorders 37 ; (4) experiencing clinically significant agitation/aggression as assessed by the NPI, for which the frequency is either ‘Very frequently’ or ‘Frequently’ and the severity is ‘moderate’ or ‘marked’ 38 ; (5) informed consent for participation in the study provided by both caregiver and participant (or surrogate consent and participant assent); (6) having an available caregiver who spends at least several hours per week with the participant; and (7) on a stable (≥7 days) dosing of antipsychotics for agitation or psychosis, if being used at all.

Exclusion criteria were as follows: (1) meeting the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders 5th Edition (DSM-5) 39 criteria for a major depressive episode (MDE) in the past 90 days; (2) residing in a skilled nursing or long-term acute care facility; (3) having a failed treatment attempt with citalopram or escitalopram for agitation after adequate trial, at minimally accepted dose; (4) QTcB as determined on EN electrocardiogram (defined as >450 ms for men and >470 ms for women); (5) treatment within 7 days of EN with any of the following: antidepressants (other than trazodone, ≤100 mg per day at bedtime), benzodiazepines (other than lorazepam) or psychostimulants; (6) recent (<14 days of EN) changes in dextromethorphan/quinidine, prazosin and pimavanserin; and (7) recent (<14 days) use of medical marijuana.

Of note, we did not use biomarker-based diagnosis. The challenges of biomarker-based diagnosis at the time would have made the study almost impossible. In this population with acute agitation, getting an amyloid positron emission tomography scan or a cerebrospinal fluid analysis would have been extremely difficult, and reliable blood biomarkers with short turnaround times were not available.

All enrolled participants signed written informed consents that were approved by a single institutional review board (Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health as the board of record), by Clinical Trials Ontario (Centre for Addiction and Mental Health as the board of record) or by local institutional review boards at the Biomedical Research Foundation, the University of Calgary and Foothills Medical Centre, the Centre for Memory and Aging, Columbia University, Roper St. Francis Healthcare and the University of California Los Angeles/VA Greater Los Angeles Healthcare System.

Randomization and masking

Using a documented program built in-house for data collection (‘Data-PRISM’) and SAS software (version 9.4) 40 , the randomization schedule was designed to yield an expected allocation ratio of 1:1 for escitalopram and placebo. Assignments were stratified by clinical site ( n = 27), and schedules employed permuted block designs, with block sizes of 2 and 4. Treatment assignments were masked to the participants and clinical site personnel through the 12 weeks after randomization. Treatments were assigned at BL. Routine unmasking occurred for all participants at 12 weeks, after the data collection was completed. Placebo tablets were manufactured to match the tablets of active drug by a pharmaceutical manufacturing service (Temple University School of Pharmacy cGMP Services).

Interventions

For those randomized to the study drug, each tablet contained 5 mg of escitalopram. The target study dose was 15 mg per day provided as a single dose (preferably in the morning). Upon randomization, participants started on 5 mg (one tablet) of escitalopram daily for 7 days. The dose was increased to 10 mg per day (two tablets) for another 7 days, after which the dose increased to 15 mg (three tablets). For those randomized to placebo, the treatment schedule and number of tablets were the same. If necessary, at any time, local study physicians had the option of decreasing the daily dose by 5 mg at a time depending on tolerability, to a minimum of 5 mg.

PSI.

All caregivers and participants were provided with a standardized PSI 41 . PSI consisted of three components: a counseling session, the provision of education materials and 24-hour availability for crises, with counseling over the telephone or video as appropriate. The education materials consisted of the same materials across clinics. However, local resource materials (such as on finding a local home health aide) were also used as needed. The counseling session, in which a trained study clinician counseled the participant and caregiver, took place at EN, at BL and at each study visit after BL. PSI took approximately 20–30 minutes. Each counseling session consisted of the following: (1) review and adjustment of the participant and caregiver supportive care plans; (2) emotional support and opportunity to express feelings; (3) counseling regarding specific caregiving skills; (4) assistance with problem solving of specific issues that the caregiver brought to the sessions; and (5) answers for questions regarding the educational materials. These PSI procedures were used in previous controlled trials for neuropsychiatric symptoms in AD 19 , 42 .

Assessments and outcomes

 Extended Data Tables 7 and 8 summarize all assessments and their schedule. The study included secondary outcome measures that will be reported in future publications and that are described in our methods publication 26 . In brief, EN activities and assessments were first completed, including signing the informed consent form and assessments of eligibility, agitation and safety. After completing the assessments and confirming eligibility, PSI was provided to the participant and caregiver and continued for 3 weeks. BL assessments occurred 3 weeks after EN assessments. At BL, ongoing consent and eligibility were verified, and assessments of symptoms and safety were completed. Eligibility for randomization was based on the BL mADCS-CGIC and the QTcB. Randomized participants were then assessed for 12 weeks. Efficacy outcomes were reported based on week 3, 6, 9 and 12 assessments, with the primary outcome timepoint being 12 weeks 26 .

COVID-19 effects and adaptations.

To minimize the risk of contracting COVID-19 from participating in the study during the pandemic, and to make the study procedures less burdensome, we implemented changes to our original protocols, with the key ones being the following: (1) converted in-person visit at 9 weeks to a telephone visit, and, consequently, given this major change in the method of data collection at this timepoint compared to 3 weeks, 6 weeks and 12 weeks, we chose to report the 9-week results in the Supplementary Information ; (2) adapt to telephone assessment of cognition by modifying an eligibility criterion from a score range (5–28) on the MMSE 36 to a score range (3–20) on the MMSET; (3) administered the mADCS-CGIC, MMSET and NPI/NPI-C solely virtually; (4) allowed for PSI to be administered by phone/video based on local preference/permission; and (5) added ‘Time-dependent covariate for study enrollment before and after the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic’ to the primary analysis.

Before the pandemic, recruitment was in-line with study targets. Despite the above-mentioned modifications intended to make the study less burdensome during the pandemic, and a series of other measures (addition of over 15 new sites in the United States and Canada and professional marketing assistance), we were ultimately not able to meet the originally planned recruitment targets.

Primary outcome.

The primary outcome was the proportion of participants who experienced clinically significant improvement in agitation, indicated as ‘moderate’ or ‘marked’ improvement on the mADCS-CGIC referencing the BL assessment at 12 weeks 26 .

Secondary outcomes.

In addition to the primary outcome measure, we assessed efficacy using the mADCS-CGIC categorical outcome, NPI-C (agitation and aggression domains), ADL and ZBI. To assess safety, we compared the two groups on the following outcome measures: ‘get-up and go’ test 43 , MMSET, QTcB and rates of adverse and serious adverse events.

Statistical analyses

All randomized participants were included in the primary analysis following the intention-to-treat (ITT) principle, treating participants who drop out or die as not having improved on the primary outcome. All events after randomization were counted. All measures were evaluated for outliers, and distributional assumptions were checked to ensure applicability of the statistical procedures. Analyses are presented as unadjusted and adjusted for clinical site (the stratification variable).

For the primary outcome analysis, the two groups were compared using Fisher’s exact test. We report the RD and 95% CI. In addition, we conducted the following sensitivity analyses: (1) a per-protocol analysis, censoring those who died, withdrew consent or were lost to follow-up; (2) classifying participants with missing outcome data as having significant improvement; (3) multiple imputation of mADCS-CGIC scores for missing outcomes; and (4) controlling for study enrollment before and after the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic.

For the secondary outcomes analyses, the two groups were compared using a proportional odds analysis using ordinal logistic regression for mADCS-CGIC as a categorical outcome. We used a chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test for non-continuous outcome measures.

All analyses were two-sided, and no corrections were made for multiple comparisons for the sensitivity or secondary analyses.

Sample size

The sample size was based on the comparison of the two groups in the RCT portion of the study. The primary outcome, upon which the sample size was based, was a score of either 1 or 2 (corresponding to ‘moderate’ or ‘marked’ improvement) on the mADCS-CGIC at the 12-week visit in reference to the BL visit, as assessed using the mADCS-CGIC. The mADCS-CGIC is a seven-point scale with measures ranging from marked improvement (score of 1) to marked worsening (score of 7). Our definition of clinically significant improvement was a binary measure indicating a score of either 1 or 2 (corresponding to ‘moderate’ or ‘marked’ improvement) on the mADCS-CGIC.

S-CitAD was designed to demonstrate efficacy at a level equal to or exceeding that found in CitAD, with the rationale being that lower efficacy could not justify the widespread use of escitalopram over citalopram for the treatment of agitation in AD. In CitAD, 40% of participants in the citalopram + PSI group demonstrated clinically significant improvement in agitation (as defined above) after 9 weeks of treatment, compared to a corresponding measure of 26% of participants in the placebo + PSI group, for a relative risk of 1.53. We calculated a sample size of n = 352 (176 per group) to have 80% power to detect an RD of 14% at 12 weeks, assuming a 26% response rate in the placebo + PSI group and setting the two-sided type I error to be 0.05. Allowing for 10% loss to follow-up, we planned to randomize 392 participants to one of the two treatment arms in the randomized portion of the study. Although a 3-week course of PSI alone was likely to be less effective than what was observed in the CitAD placebo + PSI group, we allowed for also a 26% response rate during the 3-week PSI-alone run-in phase, and the total enrollment target sample was (392 / 0.74) = 530 participants. Sample size calculations were performed using ‘PROC POWER’ for a chi-square test in SAS software, version 9.3.

Extended Data Table 7 |.

Assessments

 Assessment

 Description

 Alzheimer’s Disease Cooperative Study-Activities of Daily Living Scale (ADCS-ADL) 27 

 This measure assesses functional performance in patients with AD. In a structured interview format, informants are queried as to whether participants attempted each of 24 items in the inventory during the prior 3 weeks and their level of performance. The scale discriminates well among the stages of severity of AD, from very mild impairment to severe impairment.

 Caregiver Activity Survey (CAS) 44 

 This measure assesses the time spent caregiving or aiding participants with AD with their day-to-day activities. It consists of six items (communicating with the person, using transportation, eating, dressing, looking after one's appearance and supervising the person).

 Get up and Go Test 43 

 The Get Up and Go Test is a performance-based, sensitive measure of function in older persons. It measures the time a person takes to get up from a chair and walk 50ft (15.2m) as fast as possible along a level and unobstructed corridor. It has been used as an assessment of mobility or as an indicator of adverse functional effects of treatments.

 Mini-Mental State Examination Telephone (MMSET) 34 

 This is a 22-point validated telephone adapted version of the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE). The particular domains most often affected in AD (orientation, memory) are included in this instrument. Other items include those assessing working memory and naming. The MMSET omits the following items from the MMSE: following verbal and written commands, spontaneously writing a sentence, and copying two overlapping pentagons.

 Modified Alzheimer’s Disease Cooperative Study - Clinical Global Impression of Change (mADCS-CGIC) 20 

 The mADCS-CGIC is a systematic method, developed for the AD setting to assess clinically significant change in a patient’s agitation. A trained clinician, blind to treatment assignment and other outcome measure results, uses a 7-point Likert scale to rate each participant along a continuum from marked improvement to marked worsening, based on an interview with the caregiver and examination of the participant. The mADCS-CGIC requires the assessor to consider a number of aspects of the agitation prior to providing a “global” assessment of change. These include: emotional agitation, mood liability/distress, psychomotor agitation, verbal aggression, and physical aggression

 Neuropsychiatric Inventory (NPI) 35 

 The NPI is the most widely-used measure of neuropsychiatric symptoms in dementia clinical trials. The NPI assesses type and severity of behavioral disturbances in dementia. The full inventory evaluates 12 domains of NPS: apathy, agitation, delusions, hallucinations, depression, euphoria, aberrant motor behavior, irritability, disinhibition, anxiety, sleeping, and eating. Frequency (1 = occasionally, less than once/week; 2 = often, about once per week; 3 = frequently, more than once a week; 4 = very frequently, once or more/day or continuously) and severity (1 = mild, 2 = moderate, 3 = severe) scales in each domain are scored based on responses from an informed caregiver involved in the participant’s life. The NPI also includes a 5-point rating used to quantify caregiver distress with the participant’s neuropsychiatric symptoms. To obtain an NPI score for each domain, the severity score and the frequency score are multiplied. Caregiver distress ratings of each domain are summed. In S-CitAD, the NPI domains administered did not include agitation/aggression or dysphoria (at select visits) so as to avoid redundancy with the NPI-C domains rated (see below). The frequency by severity score was used to measure changes in behavior and psychological disturbances over time

 Neuropsychiatric Inventory – Clinician Rating (NPI-C) - the agitation, aggression, and dysphoria domains 28 

 As with the standard NPI, the NPI-C assesses type and severity of behavioral disturbances in dementia, and is used to rate the presence of neuropsychiatric symptoms across many domains, such as dysphoria or agitation. It is designed especially for clinicians to provide their structured input to the NPI rating. The agitation and aggression domain scores were calculated to measure the secondary agitation outcome, with a score range of 0 to 39 for agitation and 0 to 24 for aggression. We also used the dysphoria domain to measure depression with scores ranging from 0 to 39.

 Zarit Burden Interview (ZBI) 29 

 The ZBI is used to assess caregiver burden, via self-report questions, used in a wide variety of populations. We used the 12-item version which is highly correlated with the 22-item version, but does not include items regarding embarrassment and caregiver burden. Each item on the interview is a statement which the caregiver is asked to endorse using a 5-point scale. Response options range from 0 (Never) to 4 (Nearly Always). Domains include problem areas such as caregiver health, psychological well-being, monetary issues, socialization, and the relationship with the participant.

 Neurobehavioral Rating Scale (NBRS) 45 

 NBRS is a 28-item instrument rated by an observer and patient and derived from the Brief Psychiatric Rating Scale (BPRS) that assesses multiple types of psychopathology. It combines coverage of the breadth of psychopathology addressed in the BPRS with more comprehensive assessment of impairment seen in dementia. Scoring on the NBRS is based on a seven-point Likert scale of increasing severity (i.e. 0=not present, 1=very mild, 2-mild, 3=moderate, 4=moderately severe, 5=severe, 6=extremely severe). As in Pollock (2002), we generated a NBRS agitation domain based on NBRS items 8, 11 and 14, with a total range of 0 to 18 points. This agitation domain score has been shown to be sensitive to drug treatment effects with citalopram.

De-identified S-CitAD data are available for health-related research. Access is contingent upon approval by an appropriate institutional ethics board or equivalent and approval by the CitAD Steering Committee after review of a submitted CitAD Data Use Agreement form. Completed forms may be submitted by emailing citad@citadtrial.org .
