TITLE: Effectiveness of Sequential Psychological and Medication Therapies for Insomnia Disorder: A Randomized Clinical Trial

METHODS:
Study Design: Single-blinded, randomized clinical trial conducted at 2 sites: Institut Universitaire en Sante Mentale de Quebec, Universite Laval, Quebec City, Quebec, Canada, and National Jewish Health, Denver, Colorado. Local ethics committees approved the protocol, and all participants provided written informed consent. Enrollment took place from August 2012 through July 2017.

Design: Two treatment stages and two treatment arms (psychological therapy: behavior therapy [BT] and cognitive therapy [CT]; and medication: zolpidem and trazodone for each stage, leading to 4 treatment sequences). Participants meeting criteria (N = 211) were randomly assigned in a 1:1 ratio to BT (n = 107) or zolpidem (n = 104), stratified by sex, age (<55 years vs >=55 years), and presence of a comorbid psychiatric disorder. After the initial 6-week therapy, patients in remission were followed up for 12 months receiving maintenance therapy, while nonremitters were randomized to a second-stage therapy. Measurements taken at baseline, end of first-stage and second-stage therapies (weeks 6 and 12), and at 3-month, 6-month, and 12-month follow-ups.

Participants: 211 adults meeting criteria for insomnia disorder recruited from the community through media advertisements and outpatient clinics. Primary study end points were percentages of individuals achieving treatment response or remission using ISI total scores. Response defined as reduction of 8+ points on ISI. Remission defined as ISI score <8.

Treatments: First-stage psychological therapy = BT (sleep restriction and stimulus control). Second-stage = CT (targeting sleep-disruptive cognitions). First-stage medication = zolpidem sublingual 5-10 mg nightly (limited to 5 mg in women per FDA). Second-stage medication = trazodone 50-150 mg.

Statistical Analyses: Weighted logistic GEE models. Power analyses computed for sample size of 224 participants for first-stage randomization. Two-tailed 5% alpha, 80% power.

RESULTS:
Patients: 211 adults (132 women; mean [SD] age 45.6 [14.9] years) with insomnia disorder (mean duration 13.2 years). 74 patients (35%) had comorbid psychiatric disorder. 137 (68%) had at least 1 comorbid medical disorder. Of 211 randomized, 43 did not complete first-stage therapy (15.3% in BT vs 25.2% in zolpidem; P = .15). Of unremitted and available participants, 108 accepted second-stage randomization (27 per condition). Cumulative attrition significantly higher for sequences starting with zolpidem.

Treatment Response: Both first-stage therapies produced similar response rates (BT 45.5%; zolpidem 49.7%). Significant response increases with second-stage therapy for BT+zolpidem (40.6% to 62.7%; OR 2.46) and BT+CT (50.6% to 68.2%; OR 2.09). No significant change for sequences starting with zolpidem.

Remission Rates: No significant difference at post1 between BT (38.0%) and zolpidem (30.3%). Second-stage therapy produced significant remission increases in BT+zolpidem (38.1% to 55.9%; OR 2.06). No significant overall differences in cumulative remission at post2.

Follow-up: Improvements sustained over 12 months. Further increases in BT+zolpidem and zolpidem+BT at follow-ups.

Psychiatric Comorbidity Effects: At post1, higher response among patients without comorbidity (53.9% vs 36.4%; nonsignificant). At post2, inverse: patients with comorbidity showed significantly higher response (74.2% vs 53.6%). Patients with psychiatric comorbidity receiving same-modality sequences (BT+CT or zolpidem+trazodone) did best.

Sleep Diary: BT produced larger effects than zolpidem on SOL and WASO. Significant gains in sleep efficiency for both treatments, higher for BT (12.5% vs 5.8%).
