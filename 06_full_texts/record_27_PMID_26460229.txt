TITLE: Optimal duration of risperidone or olanzapine adjunctive therapy to mood stabilizer following remission of a manic episode: A CANMAT randomized double-blind trial

METHODS:
This study was a multi-center three-parallel-group randomized double-blind placebo-controlled trial with up to 52 weeks of follow-up conducted across 17 CANMAT-affiliated academic centers in Canada and collaborating sites in Brazil.

Subjects: Patients aged 17 years or older were eligible if they had: (1) Bipolar I disorder diagnosis; (2) treatment within the previous 12 weeks for a DSM-IV acute manic or mixed episode with mood stabilizer (lithium or valproate) and atypical antipsychotic (risperidone or olanzapine); (3) remission from the manic or mixed episode for at least 2 weeks and no more than 6 weeks based on Clinical Global Impression Severity score of 2 or less for 2 consecutive weeks or Young Mania Rating Scale score of 8 or less and Hamilton Rating Scale for Depression 21-item score of 8 or less for two consecutive weeks. Patients with comorbid substance abuse or other axis I disorders were allowed but those taking other psychotropic medication with the exception of benzodiazepines were excluded.

Trial Design and Interventions: After obtaining informed consent, patients were randomized to one of three groups. Randomizations were stratified by drug combination and by center. The treating clinicians and all research personnel except the trial statistician and the pharmacist were blinded to treatment arm allocations.

Patients randomized to the '0-weeks' group tapered and discontinued risperidone or olanzapine over 2 weeks beginning on the day of randomization and received placebo substitution for the remaining 50 weeks. Patients randomized to the '24-weeks' group received risperidone or olanzapine for 24 weeks, then tapered over 2 weeks with placebo substitution for the remaining 26 weeks. Patients randomized to the '52-weeks' group continued risperidone or olanzapine for 52 weeks.

All patients continued the same mood stabilizer they had been taking at study entry with serum levels maintained within therapeutic ranges (0.6-1.2 mmol/L for lithium and 350-830 umol/L for valproate) throughout the 52 weeks. The dose and type of atypical antipsychotic remained the same as at entry. Allowed dosages: risperidone 1-6 mg per day and olanzapine 5-25 mg per day. Patients were not allowed other psychotropic medication except benzodiazepines for sedation and anti-parkinsonian medication for extrapyramidal side effects. Psychoeducation and counseling regarding sleep hygiene, healthy daily routines and rhythms, alcohol and substance abuse, anxiety management, conflict resolution and problem solving were allowed as clinically indicated.

Study Outcomes: Patients were assessed biweekly until week 8 and every 4 weeks thereafter up to week 52 by trained raters blind to treatment allocation using: Young Mania Rating Scale, Hamilton Depression Rating Scale-21, Montgomery-Asberg Depression Rating Scale, Clinical Global Impression-Severity and Clinical Global Impression-Bipolar Severity. Clinical Global Impression-Improvement and CGI-BP change were assessed from week 2 onwards. Safety and tolerability were evaluated through clinical observation and the Udvalg for Kliniske Undersogelser Side Effect Rating Scale and Extrapyramidal Symptoms Rating Scale at baseline and monthly visits. Laboratory measurements on blood sera were obtained at screening visit and weeks 12, 24, 36 and 52.

Primary outcome measure was time to any mood episode, defined as: (i) Young Mania Rating Scale score of 15 or greater; (ii) Hamilton Depression Rating Scale 21-item score of 15 or greater or suicide item score of 3 or greater; (iii) Clinical Global Impression-Severity score of 3 or greater; (iv) hospitalization for treatment of mood symptoms or (v) suicide or suicide attempt. Patients experiencing a primary event were removed from further study follow-up.

Secondary outcome measures were time to a manic episode, time to a depressive episode and time to premature discontinuation from the study for any clinical reason.

Sample Size: The planned sample size for the trial was 540 patients (180 per group). This sample size was based on two primary comparisons of the event proportions (24-weeks vs 0-weeks groups and 52-weeks vs 0-week groups) by 52 weeks, each at a two-sided significance level of 0.025 (to ensure an overall type I error rate less than 5%) with 80% power and allowing for a 25% drop-out rate. The assumed event proportions were 55% in the 0-week group and 38% (absolute reduction of 17%) in each of the other two groups.

Statistical Analysis: All analyses were conducted on an intent-to-treat basis and included all randomized patients. Kaplan-Meier cumulative incidence plots were used to summarize the time to any mood episode by treatment group. For the primary analysis, a Cox proportional hazards model with adjustment for the antipsychotic drug and for the mood stabilizer drug was used to compare the time to any mood episode across treatment groups. As a sensitivity analysis, a mixed effects (frailty) Cox model with site entered as a clustering variable was fit to account for potential site effects. Similar analyses evaluated time to manic and depressive episodes. Changes in weight and laboratory parameters were calculated as the difference between baseline and last follow-up measurements.

RESULTS:
Patients and Disposition: A total of 159 patients were randomized (52 to the 0-weeks group, 54 to the 24-weeks group and 53 to the 52-weeks group) into the trial across 17 sites. The recruitment for the study was much slower than anticipated and ultimately was stopped because of expiration of funding.

Patient characteristics were well-balanced across groups except: patients in the 0-weeks group had a higher percentage of women, lower mean weight and lower percentage with psychiatric comorbidity compared with other groups; patients in the 24-weeks group had a lower percentage with alcohol/substance abuse. A total of 34 (21%) patients discontinued the study (8, 14 and 12 patients in the 0-weeks, 24-weeks and 52-weeks groups, respectively) for reasons other than meeting the primary endpoint. Mean follow-up times were 18, 25 and 24 weeks for the 0-weeks, 24-weeks and 52-weeks groups, respectively.

Primary Outcomes: There were 39 primary events (depression=25, mania=14) among 52 patients in the 0-weeks group, 29 events (depression=23, mania=6) among 54 patients in the 24-weeks group and 29 (depression=22, mania=7) events among 53 patients in the 52-weeks group. Kaplan-Meier cumulative incidence curves suggested time to any mood episode was longer in both 52-weeks and 24-weeks groups compared with the 0-weeks group. Time to any mood episode was similar between the 52-weeks and 24-weeks groups.

In adjusted Cox analysis, hazard ratio for time to any mood episode was 0.53 for the 24-weeks group relative to the 0-weeks group (95% CI: 0.33, 0.86; P=0.01) and 0.63 for the 52-weeks group relative to the 0-weeks group (95% CI: 0.39, 1.02; P=0.06). The hazard ratio for the 52-weeks group relative to the 24-weeks group was 1.18 (95% CI: 0.71, 1.99; P=0.52). On the basis of Kaplan-Meier curves, the estimated 52-week event rates were 65%, 65% and 87% in the 52-weeks, 24-weeks and 0-weeks groups, respectively.

Secondary Outcomes: Across groups, a total of 27 patients met criteria for a manic event and 70 for a depressive event. Time to a manic episode was longer in patients randomized to the 24-weeks and the 52-weeks groups compared with the 0-weeks group and was statistically significant for the 24-weeks group (HR: 0.30, 95% CI: 0.12, 0.80; P=0.02) but not for the 52-weeks group (HR: 0.43, 95% CI: 0.17, 1.09; P=0.08). Although time to a depressive episode was also longer in the 24-weeks and 52-weeks groups compared with the 0-weeks group, this difference was not statistically significant for either group. Time to discontinuation for any clinical reason was longer in the 24-weeks and the 52-weeks groups compared with the 0-weeks group, but effect was statistically significant only for the 24-weeks group (HR: 0.54, 95% CI: 0.34, 0.86, P=0.01).

Subgroup Analysis (Olanzapine): Time to any mood episode was longer in both the 24-weeks and the 52-weeks groups compared with the 0-weeks group, with difference reaching statistical significance for the 52-week group (HR: 0.23, 95% CI: 0.09, 0.59, P=0.003).

Subgroup Analysis (Risperidone): Time to any mood episode was longer in the 24-weeks group relative to 0-weeks group but was not statistically significant (HR: 0.57, 95% CI: 0.31, 1.05, P=0.07). Time to any mood episode in the 52-week group was similar to that in the 0-weeks group (HR: 1.05, 95% CI: 0.59, 1.88, P=0.86).

Adverse Events: The only serious adverse event recorded was a single death from pneumonia in the 52-weeks group, determined by the site investigator as unrelated to the study medication. Patients in the 52-weeks group gained significantly more weight (3.2 kg, P=0.01) compared with those in the 0-weeks group (lost 0.2 kg) and 24-weeks group (lost 0.1 kg). Clinically significant weight gain (7% or more of baseline weight) was more common among patients in the 52-weeks group (25%) than in the 0-weeks (12%) and 24-weeks (15%) groups.
