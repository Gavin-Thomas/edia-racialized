Key Points

Question  Does the PETRUSHKA tool, a web-based clinical decision-support system combining clinical and demographic predictors with patient preferences to personalize antidepressant treatment, improve clinical outcomes of people with major depressive disorder?

Findings  In this international, randomized clinical trial including 520 adults with major depressive disorder, patients who received a personalized antidepressant selected using the PETRUSHKA tool were less likely to discontinue their treatment due to any reason at 8 weeks compared with participants treated by their clinicians under usual care conditions (41/241 [17%] vs 69/252 [27%], respectively; adjusted relative risk, 0.62 [95% CI, 0.44-0.88]; P = .007).

Meaning  In adults with major depressive disorder, the PETRUSHKA tool was effective in personalizing antidepressant treatment and increased the likelihood of continuing treatment through 8 weeks.

 A Decision-Support System to Personalize Antidepressant Treatment in Major Depressive Disorder
Visual Abstract.

Importance  Antidepressants for moderate to severe major depressive disorder may be discontinued prematurely because the prescribed antidepressant is not always the most appropriate medication for an individual. Guidelines have recommended more precise targeting of antidepressant treatment.

Objective  To evaluate the efficacy of a web-based tool to personalize antidepressant treatment.

Design, Setting, and Participants  This multicenter, randomized clinical trial included persons between the ages of 18 and 74 years with major depressive disorder. The trial was conducted at 47 sites in 3 countries (Brazil, Canada, and the UK). The first participant was screened on November 29, 2022, and the last follow-up visit occurred on January 15, 2025.

Intervention  A total of 540 participants were randomized (1:1) to an evidence-based clinical decision-support system (PETRUSHKA tool; n = 271) or usual care (n = 269).

Main Outcomes and Measures  The primary outcome was treatment discontinuation due to any cause at 8 weeks. The secondary outcomes included treatment discontinuation up to 24 weeks due to adverse events and changes in depressive symptoms (measured with the 9-item Patient Health Questionnaire [PHQ-9]; range, 0-27; higher scores indicate more severe depression) and anxiety symptoms (measured with the 7-item Generalized Anxiety Disorder [GAD-7] questionnaire; range, 0-21; higher scores indicate more severe symptoms).

Results  Of the 520 eligible participants, 493 were included in the primary analysis (median age, 35 [IQR, 25 to 48] years; 58% female; PHQ-9 mean score, 16.6 [SD, 5.1]; GAD-7 mean score, 11.5 [SD, 4.1]). At 8 weeks, 41 of 241 participants (17%) in the PETRUSHKA group discontinued the prescribed antidepressant due to any cause vs 69 of 252 (27%) in the usual care group (adjusted relative risk, 0.62 [95% CI, 0.44 to 0.88]; P = .007). At 8 weeks, 22 of 241 participants (9%) in the PETRUSHKA group discontinued the prescribed antidepressant due to adverse events vs 39 of 252 (16%) in the usual care group (adjusted relative risk, 0.59 [95% CI, 0.36 to 0.97]; P = .04). For the assessment of depressive symptoms at 24 weeks, the mean PHQ-9 score was 7.1 (SD, 5.4) in the PETRUSHKA group vs 9.2 (SD, 6.5) in the usual care group (n = 129 in each group; adjusted between-group mean difference, −1.92 [95% CI, −3.06 to −0.78]; P < .001). For the assessment of anxiety symptoms at 24 weeks, the mean GAD-7 score was 4.6 (SD, 4.1) in the PETRUSHKA group (n = 133) vs 5.8 (SD, 4.9) in the usual care group (n = 126) (adjusted between-group mean difference, −1.39 [95% CI, −2.26 to −0.52]; P = .002).

Conclusions and Relevance  Compared with usual care, use of the PETRUSHKA tool increased the number of patients still taking their antidepressant at 8 weeks and improved depressive and anxiety symptoms at 24 weeks. However, lack of a double-blind design and the large amount of missing data limit the validity of these results.

Introduction

With 280 million people currently affected in the world, major depressive disorder (MDD) is one of the leading causes of global health burden, and it is forecast to increase further by 2050.1 Antidepressants are recommended as one of the first-line options for people with moderate to severe symptoms2; however, a key challenge is how to best implement these treatments in clinical practice. Prescription of antidepressants has increased over the last 25 years,3 but the majority of people with MDD take them for too short a period.4 The initial adverse events associated with antidepressant use contribute to the suboptimal duration of treatment, reducing the clinical effectiveness of these medications.5 This is exacerbated by the inability of clinicians to reliably predict which antidepressant will cause adverse events in each individual patient, and which medication will be the most effective for each patient. Although guidelines advocate personalized treatment decisions, implementation in practice remains limited.

In some fields of medicine, such as oncology and cardiology, prediction tools play an important role in clinical decision-making.6,7 However, in psychiatry, prediction of treatment outcomes is challenging,8 especially for patients with MDD, because treatment trajectories are influenced by the complex etiology and heterogeneous symptomatology of the disorder.9 Previous studies have found that sociodemographic features (eg, age, biological sex, marital status) and clinical characteristics (eg, symptom severity, prior treatments, comorbidities) are associated with individual treatment outcomes in MDD. However, there is no consensus on which pretreatment characteristics can accurately predict efficacy and acceptability of specific antidepressants. Moreover, the acceptance of adverse events by patients is heterogeneous and some patients may be more willing to tolerate certain adverse events over others based on their personal circumstances.

As part of the PETRUSHKA (Personalising Antidepressant Treatment for Unipolar Depression Combining Individual Choices, Risks and Big Data) project, we developed an online, evidence-based, decision-support system for patients and clinicians to enable personalized antidepressant treatment decision-making for patients with MDD treated in routine clinical practice.10 The PETRUSHKA tool is based on prediction models that combine statistical and machine learning methods to identify which antidepressants work better for each individual patient, which varies depending on personal characteristics (including age, sex, ethnicity, socioeconomic status, body mass index, smoking status, severity of depressive symptoms, past use of antidepressants, childhood maltreatment, anxiety, comorbid medical conditions, and use of other nonpsychiatric medications) and patient preferences. In this randomized clinical trial, we compared the PETRUSHKA tool with usual care.

Methods

This randomized clinical trial was performed in accordance with the principles of Good Clinical Practice and the Declaration of Helsinki.11 The trial protocol and the statistical analysis plan appear in Supplement 1. All participants provided written or electronic informed consent. This report followed the Consolidated Standards of Reporting Trials (CONSORT) reporting guideline.12 The protocol deviations appear in eTable 1 in Supplement 2. This study received approval from the South Central–Hampshire B research ethics committee.

Trial Population

Eligible patients from primary and secondary care were between the ages of 18 and 74 years with a clinical diagnosis of MDD, had an indication for pharmacological treatment, and were willing to take an antidepressant as monotherapy. Exclusion criteria included a diagnosis of treatment-resistant depression, being pregnant, having arrhythmias or QT prolongation, and requiring urgent mental health care or hospital admission (Supplement 1). Information about race and ethnicity was self-reported using fixed categories.

Trial Design, Treatments, and Assessments

The PETRUSHKA trial was an international, multicenter, superiority randomized clinical trial conducted at 47 sites in both primary care (in Brazil and in the UK) and secondary care (in Canada). Patients were randomly assigned (1:1) to the PETRUSHKA tool or usual care via a secured randomization system (Sortition version 2.3; University of Oxford), using randomly permuted blocks of different sizes and stratified according to severity of illness and recruiting center (Brazil and Canada were each considered as one separate recruiting center and the recruiting center was defined by the Integrated Care Board in the UK). Treatment group allocation was known to the patients and the treating clinicians. However, the rating scale assessors and statisticians were masked to treatment group allocation. During the baseline visit and before randomization, the clinician entered in the study database the name of the antidepressant they would prescribe to each participant under usual care conditions. This database record was created to ensure the clinician’s decision would not be affected by the outcome of the randomization. The diagnosis of MDD was formally confirmed with the revised version of the Clinical Interview Schedule, which is a standardized self-administered computerized assessment based on the International Statistical Classification of Diseases and Related Health Problems, Tenth Revision.13

For participants randomized to usual care (control group), the clinician prescribed the antidepressant they had chosen before randomization. By contrast, in the experimental group, the PETRUSHKA tool was used to select the antidepressant with the best profile for each individual.10 Based on the participant’s individual demographic and clinical characteristics, the PETRUSHKA tool predicted the performance of each drug for each outcome. These predictions were then combined with the patient’s preferences about avoiding particular adverse events to create a short list of the 3 best antidepressants (Figure 1).14 -17 The development pipeline for the PETRUSHKA prediction model appears in Supplement 1. The tool was codeveloped with people with lived experience, taking into account diverse literacy levels and digital skills. Follow-up visits were scheduled at 4, 8, and 24 weeks after randomization.

The full description of the tool has been published elsewhere.10 The tool enables real-time shared decision-making because the clinician logs into the PETRUSHKA web app and invites the patient with MDD. The clinician and the patient simultaneously access the tool from any internet-connected device either in the same consultation room or remotely.

The patient interacts with the tool in parts A through C. In D, the tool generates a personalized ranked list of recommended antidepressants with an overall score (pictogram) along with a breakdown of the actual probability for the patient to experience each of the 5 adverse events that they selected in A. The list of recommended antidepressants in D will inform the discussion between the clinician and the patient and will lead to the final treatment selection.

The version of the tool included in this study only included treatments that had been assessed in randomized clinical trials.14 The foundation of the tool is based on prediction models that used a combination of advanced statistical and machine learning methods15 and electronic health record data from adults with MDD taking antidepressants for depression. The tool incorporates preferences from patients and clinicians elicited in real time, with a specific focus on common nonserious adverse events.16,17

The web-based platform for the tool was developed using Django version 4.1.1 (a Python-based free and open-source framework). The tool was also translated into Brazilian Portuguese. Study Outcomes

The primary outcome was treatment discontinuation due to any cause at 8 weeks. This binary outcome indicates whether the participant was still taking the antidepressant prescribed at randomization (the dosage could be altered but the antidepressant should remain the same; if participants switched to another antidepressant or were taking no medication, it was considered a treatment discontinuation).

The prespecified secondary outcomes were discontinuation from treatment due to adverse events and self-rated change in depressive symptoms (9-item Patient Health Questionnaire [PHQ-9])18 and anxiety symptoms (7-item Generalized Anxiety Disorder [GAD-7] questionnaire)19 at 8 and 24 weeks. We also assessed symptoms using observer-rated scales for depression (17-item Hamilton Depression Rating Scale [HAM-D])20 and anxiety (Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale [HAM-A]).21 Throughout the study, we collected information about the risk of suicidality (Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale [C-SSRS]),22 health-related quality of life (EuroQol 5-dimension 5-level questionnaire, including the EuroQol visual analog scale),23 and adverse events.

Statistical Analysis

All-cause treatment discontinuation at 8 weeks was considered clinically relevant with a discontinuation rate of 35% in the control (usual care) group and an absolute between-group difference of 15%.24 If 252 patients were enrolled in each group, the trial would have 95% power (Supplement 1).

Participants were analyzed according to their allocated intervention group. Participants found to be ineligible after randomization were excluded from the analysis. We used separate binomial mixed-effects generalized linear models with a logit link function at 8 and 24 weeks. We derived the adjusted relative risk (RR)25 and calculated the corresponding 95% CIs with delta-method SEs.26 A sensitivity analysis of the primary outcome using multiple imputation of all time points simultaneously was carried out, and the results were pooled using the Rubin rule.27 The analysis of the binary outcomes was performed using a logistic regression model. Marginal RRs were calculated from the model. The RRs are presented in this article instead of odds ratios to facilitate the interpretation of the results. The continuous outcomes were analyzed using a linear mixed-effects regression model and an unstructured covariance structure. The adjusted mean differences (MDs) with 95% CIs and P values were calculated.

The statistical analyses were performed using Stata version 18.0 (StataCorp). The analysis codes are freely available online (https://github.com/PC-CTU1/PETRUSHKA) and the full details appear in Supplement 1.

Results
Trial Population

Between November 29, 2022, and July 31, 2024, 540 patients were randomized and enrolled; however, 20 were found to be ineligible (18 in the PETRUSHKA group and 2 in the usual care group) after randomization and were excluded from the analysis (Figure 2 and eFigure 1 in Supplement 2). The baseline characteristics were similar in the 2 groups (Table 1 and eTables 2-3 in Supplement 2). Of the 520 eligible participants, 493 were included in the primary analysis (median age, 35 [IQR, 25 to 48] years; 300 [58%] were female). The baseline severity of depression symptoms was reflected by the PHQ-9 mean score of 16.6 (SD, 5.1) and the HAM-D mean score of 16.3 (SD, 5.2). The baseline severity of anxiety symptoms was reflected by the GAD-7 mean score of 11.5 (SD, 4.1) and the HAM-A mean score of 18.9 (SD, 7.4). The majority (84%) were White patients (437 of 520) and the median body mass index (calculated as weight in kilograms divided by height in meters squared) was 26.6 (IQR, 23.0-30.8). The baseline characteristics by country appear in eTable 4 in Supplement 2.

Of the participants who discontinued treatment at 8 weeks, 15 in the PETRUSHKA group switched to another antidepressant compared with 27 in the usual care group. When measured in primary care only, there was a larger effect in favor of the PETRUSHKA tool for treatment discontinuation at 8 weeks due to any cause (36/226 [16%] in the PETRUSHKA group vs 66/234 [28%] in the usual care group; adjusted RR, 0.57 [95% CI, 0.39-0.81]; P = .002) (eTable 7 and eFigure 2 in Supplement 2).

Efficacy

Although there were no between-group differences at 8 weeks in both self-rated depression and anxiety symptoms, there were differences at 24 weeks. For depressive symptoms assessed with the PHQ-9 score, the mean score was 7.1 (SD, 5.4) in the PETRUSHKA group vs 9.2 (SD, 6.5) in the usual care group (n = 129 in each group) (adjusted between-group MD, −1.92 [95% CI, −3.06 to −0.78]; P < .001). For anxiety symptoms assessed with the GAD-7, the mean score was 4.6 (SD, 4.1) in the PETRUSHKA group (n = 133) vs 5.8 (SD, 4.9) in the usual care group (n = 126) (adjusted between-group MD, −1.39 [95% CI, −2.26 to −0.52]; P = .002) (Table 2). For the observer-rated scales of depression and anxiety, the direction of the effect favored the PETRUSHKA tool, but there was large uncertainty (Table 2 and eTable 8 in Supplement 2).

At week 8, there was no evidence of a between-group difference in terms of health-related quality of life assessed with the EuroQol visual analog scale (mean score of 65.6 [SD, 20.8] in the PETRUSHKA group vs 68.6 [SD, 18.7] in the usual care group [n = 163 in each group]; adjusted between-group MD, −1.65 [95% CI, −2.05 to 5.36]). However, at 24 weeks there was strong evidence that the PETRUSHKA tool was better than usual care in terms of health-related quality of life assessed with the EuroQol visual analog scale (mean score of 70.3 [SD, 19.2] in the PETRUSHKA group [n = 128] vs 66.8 [SD, 20.6] in the usual care group [n = 125]; adjusted between-group MD, −4.28 [95% CI, −8.37 to −0.20], P = .04; Table 2). No evidence of a difference was found for the other secondary outcomes (Table 2 and eTable 8 and eFigure 3 in Supplement 2). We also conducted a series of sensitivity analyses to examine the effects of missing data with multiple imputation on the primary and secondary outcomes (eTables 9-11 and eFigures 4-5 in Supplement 2). The results were not materially different with multiple imputation for the primary outcome (adjusted RR, 0.63 [95% CI, 0.45 to 0.89]; P = .009).

Safety

There were 2 serious adverse events in the PETRUSHKA group; neither was judged to be caused by the PETRUSHKA tool or the prescribed antidepressant. One participant died because of undiagnosed metastatic breast cancer. Another participant was hospitalized for an overdose without suicidal intent; after hospital discharge, the patient restarted escitalopram, which was the antidepressant selected at randomization.

Discussion

Compared with usual care, participants with MDD whose antidepressant was selected using the PETRUSHKA tool had a reduced risk by approximately 40% of discontinuing their antidepressant during the first 8 weeks of treatment.

The primary outcome was selected in consultation with people with lived experience. It is a pragmatic measure of effectiveness, combining efficacy and tolerability and reflecting a range of features, including lack of clinical improvement, concerns about adverse events, and failure to perceive a benefit.28 For mental health disorders, staying on a treatment for a longer time can be considered a success in as much as it leads to greater reductions in symptoms, fewer hospitalizations, or improved quality of life.29-31 However, all-cause treatment discontinuation is not a direct and specific measure of efficacy or tolerability per se. Thus, we supplemented all-cause discontinuation (primary outcome) with an evaluation of individual clinical outcomes of efficacy, safety, quality of life, and treatment adherence (secondary outcomes) at 8 and 24 weeks.

We found that the PETRUSHKA tool was more effective than usual care when used in primary care in Brazil and the UK compared with in a psychiatric setting in Canada. A psychiatrist’s experience with a wider range of antidepressants and knowledge about specific mechanisms of action may allow them to better tailor antidepressant treatment.32 We cannot generalize these findings because we recruited patients in secondary care only from one academic hospital.33 However, our results suggest that the use of the PETRUSHKA tool could be ideally placed in clinical settings where MDD is managed by other clinicians at community hospitals and specialist outpatient clinics (neurology and cardiology clinics or another specialty), in low-resource or rural areas, and in low- and middle-income countries.

To our knowledge, the PETRUSHKA tool is the first coproduced, evidence-based, shared-decision support system to personalize treatment in depression.34 We systematically reviewed the available literature using study-level and individual-patient data from multiple sources to make predictions for relative antidepressant effects.35 We developed a bespoke approach to analyze information from randomized evidence and real-world data, combining individual-patient data network meta-regression and prognostic modeling,8 while using both machine learning and traditional statistical models.15 We then combined our predictions with individual patient preferences to calculate an overall recommendation score for each antidepressant based on multicriteria decision analysis.10

When calculating the sample size for the trial, we hypothesized an absolute between-group difference of 15%. Starting from an all-cause discontinuation rate of 35% across all antidepressants,24 we expected the PETRUSHKA tool to reduce the discontinuation rate to 20% (corresponding to an RR of 0.42). In the current trial, the all-cause discontinuation rate was 17% (rather than hypothesized 15%) in the PETRUSHKA group and 28% (rather than the hypothesized 35%) in the usual care group, reducing the observed absolute between-group difference to about 11%. Notwithstanding this smaller difference in absolute rates, the RR reduction of 0.38 was close to the originally hypothesized RR reduction of 0.42, and this difference does not affect our clinical interpretation of the findings.

We do not know if the favorable results with use of the PETRUSHKA tool are due to the prediction (ie, the recommended treatment is actually a better match for the patient), the elicitation of patient preferences (ie, patients may be more motivated to continue the prescribed medicine if they are informed and actively involved in its selection), or a combination of both. A factorial design would be needed to assess separate and combined effects of prediction and personalization, which would have required a substantially larger sample size. There is a growing trend of incorporating the patient perspective into clinical management and health care decision-making, which may improve care and reduce costs.36 However, most clinical decisions—especially in mental health—are still made by physicians with little input from patients.37 Interactive web-based tools (such as PETRUSHKA), which combine clinical evidence with individual preferences, can reduce conflict and better prepare individuals for decision-making.38 It is crucial to develop and validate clinical decision support tools that are simple to implement, transparent, and credible.39

In this trial, we demonstrated that antidepressant treatment can be optimized for individuals by using sociodemographic and clinical predictors. Recent studies have shown that pharmacogenomics can improve antidepressant response and reduce adverse effects in MDD.40,41 Polygenic scores can also contribute useful information about risk.42 Advanced DNA sequencing technologies now exist, which are suitable for use in low-resource environments, and return results in less than 72 hours.43 Hence, pharmacogenomic variants and polygenic scores can provide timely individual-level information (especially about adverse effects) that can be used to further personalize treatments.44,45 Moreover, they could help identify patient subgroups with specific molecular characteristics that can inform animal model studies and ultimately support drug discovery in neuroscience.46

Limitations

This study has some limitations. First, we used an open design for patients and clinicians. A double-blind trial would have been, in theory, the best study design to address our research question.47 However, it was not ethically possible to do so because the PETRUSHKA tool would have produced personalized recommendations, whereas the placebo tool would have produced random recommendations that could have harmed the participants. We extensively engaged with people with lived experiences of depression, ethicists, and international experts in the field and decided that usual care was the best comparator for this study. To reduce the risk of ascertainment bias of an unblinded design, assessors and statisticians were not aware of treatment allocation. However, when evaluating mood and anxiety symptoms, we found that the observer-rated scales did not find any meaningful between-group difference at 24 weeks, whereas the self-rated questionnaires did find meaningful between-group differences. This apparent discrepancy is not unique to mental health.48,49 However, self-rating scales provide important additional information for therapy evaluation that is different from information from observer ratings because they measure the patient’s perception of their own illness and recovery in a more holistic way.50 Another issue related to the lack of blinding is the possibility of disappointment effects.51 Patients volunteering to participate in a trial of personalized treatment selection who were then assigned to usual care may be more likely to discontinue treatment and experience poorer outcomes than they would have experienced outside the trial.

Second, we used individual rather than cluster randomization. However, we did not find any evidence of contamination (eg, different antidepressants recommended in the 2 groups) at the end of the trial.

Third, the PETRUSHKA tool did not include information about all available antidepressants. We contacted pharmaceutical companies for patient-level data but did not receive data for some of the drugs (eg, reboxetine) and only a small amount of data for other drugs (eg, sertraline). We hope that the positive results from the current trial will encourage researchers and industry to share all available evidence about antidepressants so that future versions of the tool will include information on all the drugs used in clinical practice.

Fourth, we focused only on acute treatment with antidepressant monotherapy. This is clinically important because the sooner antidepressant treatment is optimized, the sooner patients can benefit from treatment.52 However, we did not include other effective interventions, such as psychotherapy, and the consequences of antidepressant treatment beyond the acute phase should also be considered, especially when patients are taking drugs with potential metabolic or cardiovascular adverse effects (mirtazapine or amitriptyline).53

Fifth, we recruited participants from 3 countries with different health care systems, increasing the external validity of our findings, but the racial majority was composed of White participants.

Sixth, there was a lot of missing information for the secondary outcomes. We contacted participants, clinicians, and recruiting sites to retrieve missing information; however, due to the real-world design of the trial, we were mainly able to retrieve additional data for the outcomes at 8 weeks. Multiple imputation and sensitivity analyses were performed and the results were in line with the primary findings; however, we cannot exclude the possibility that the benefit of the PETRUSHKA tool reduces over time.

Seventh, we acknowledge the limited interpretability of how specific demographic and clinical characteristics are combined with individual preferences, leading to different antidepressant recommendations, and may be a concern for clinicians and patients. To address this potential barrier to adoption of digital technology,39 we presented real clinical scenarios in which changing participant’s characteristics or preferences changed the ranking of the recommended antidepressants (eTable 12 in Supplement 2).

Conclusions

Compared with usual care, use of the PETRUSHKA tool increased the number of patients still taking their antidepressant at 8 weeks and improved depressive and anxiety symptoms at 24 weeks. However, lack of a double-blind design and the large amount of missing data limit the validity of these results.
